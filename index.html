Why it matters

8 reasons to build your Vansha Vruksham today

Not just for memories — for your children's health, marriages, identity, and connection to their roots.

1 🧬
Protect your family from hidden genetic diseases
Many serious conditions — thalassemia, sickle cell disease, congenital heart defects, certain hearing and vision disorders — are recessive genetic conditions. This means both parents must carry the faulty gene for their child to be affected. When parents are closely related, they are far more likely to carry the same faulty gene without knowing it.

A family tree makes these patterns visible. If thalassemia appears in one branch, a doctor can screen the other branch before marriage or pregnancy.
📊 Research finding: Children of first-cousin marriages face a 4–6% risk of autosomal recessive genetic disorders, compared to 2–3% for unrelated parents. India has approximately 100,000 new thalassemia-affected children born each year — many from preventable consanguineous unions.
2 💍
Know before marriage if two families share blood
In large joint families — especially across Telugu, Tamil, and other South Indian communities — cousin marriages are traditional and accepted. But with families spread across cities, countries, and generations, it is increasingly easy to lose track of who is related to whom.

A digital family tree lets both families check for shared ancestry in seconds, before marriage discussions begin. This is not about stigma — it is about making an informed, empowered choice.
📊 About 10% of Indian marriages involve blood relatives (Business Standard, 2026). Awareness of genetic risks remains low, and many families have a false sense of security because earlier children appeared healthy — but the underlying genetic risk does not disappear.
3 🏛️
Preserve your ancestral village, stories and culture
Your great-grandmother knew the name of every family in her village. Your grandfather knew the history of your gotra going back ten generations. That knowledge is dying with them.

Within one generation, the names of ancestral villages, traditional occupations, family legends, and cultural rituals vanish if not recorded. Vanshavali is a living archive — the first time your family's story is written down so your great-grandchildren can read it.
📖 3 generations = ~75 years. Without a written record, the names and stories of great-grandparents are almost entirely forgotten. You are the last generation who knows your grandparents' stories. This is the right moment to write them down.
4 🧭
Give your children a clear sense of who they are
Research consistently shows that children who know their family history have stronger emotional resilience, higher self-esteem, and a clearer sense of identity. Knowing "I am from the Bharadwaj gotra, my dada ji was a farmer in Karimnagar, my nana ji fought in 1947" gives a child an anchor.

Your family tree is not just a chart — it is the story your grandchildren will tell their grandchildren.
🔬 The "Do You Know?" scale — a well-studied psychological measure — shows that children who know their family history score significantly higher on resilience and well-being during times of stress and uncertainty.
5 🩺
Your family history is your most powerful medical record
Diabetes, heart disease, hypertension, certain cancers — all have a genetic component. When a doctor knows that your father, his father, and his father all had Type 2 diabetes, they can start screening 20 years earlier and advise lifestyle changes before the disease appears.

The family tree turns inherited risk into preventable outcomes. Most people don't know their grandfather's cause of death. Many lives could be saved if they did.
🩺 Family medical history is recognized by medical organizations worldwide as the single most cost-effective screening tool available — more predictive than expensive genetic tests for common conditions like heart disease, diabetes, and certain cancers.
6 ⭐
Preserve Nakshatra and Gotra — the original family record system
India's ancient rishis created the gotra system as exactly this — a way to track patrilineal descent and prevent marriages between close relatives. The Nakshatra at birth was recorded by pandits for exactly the same reason.

But this knowledge is disappearing from family memory. Kundlis are lost, pandits have passed away, ancestral records are not digitised. Vanshavali preserves the gotra, the Nakshatra, and the lineage permanently — the way it was always meant to be kept.
🕉️ The gotra system was India's original genetic registry — designed 3,000+ years ago to prevent close-relative marriages within the same patrilineal line. Preserving it digitally is restoring an ancient practice with modern tools.
7 🌍
Reconnect scattered families across countries and generations
35 million Indians live outside India. Families have branches in the US, UK, UAE, Singapore, Australia — and many cousins have never met. Third-generation NRI children often don't know their Indian relatives at all.

A shared digital family tree becomes the meeting point — where a cousin in California can see their great-grandmother's name, and a relative in Hyderabad can see the whole family including those who moved abroad decades ago.
🌏 35.4 million Indians live outside India (Ministry of External Affairs, 2024). Many second- and third-generation NRIs have lost touch with their Indian family. A shared tree is the bridge.
8 ⚖️
Resolve inheritance and legal heir disputes with clarity
Property disputes between relatives are among the most common legal conflicts in India. Ancestral property cases often drag on for decades because no one can prove who is whose child, who preceded whom, or who the legal heirs are.

A well-documented family tree — with birth years, places, and relationships — is powerful evidence. Vanshavali will offer a legally formatted heir chart as a paid feature for exactly this purpose.
⚖️ India's courts handle millions of inheritance and succession disputes every year. A documented family tree with dates and relationships is among the most useful documents a family can have for property and succession matters.
🕉️
The gotra system — India's 3,000-year genetic registry
Our ancestors understood genetics before the word existed. The gotra system was their solution — and it still works.
Same gotra = same patrilineal ancestor. The rule "never marry within the same gotra" exists because two people of the same gotra share a common male ancestor — meaning they share genes. Avoiding this marriage reduces the risk of recessive genetic disease expression in children.
Nakshatra was the birth record. Before civil registration systems, the Nakshatra at birth (recorded by a family pandit) was a person's official identifier — used in marriage matching, legal identity, and religious ceremonies. Preserving it digitally continues this tradition.
The knowledge is disappearing. As the generation that knew these things ages and passes, and as nuclear families replace joint families, this ancient knowledge is being lost. Many people today do not know their own gotra. Vanshavali exists to preserve it.
